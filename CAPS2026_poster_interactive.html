Context & motivation

Why scale citizen science?

Citizen science has proven its value across scientific disciplines — collecting new, complementary data that enhances existing monitoring methods. By scaling citizen science efforts to national, European or global levels, we can harness the power of multinational communities to tackle pressing global challenges. Yet most projects begin at small scale and face significant barriers when attempting to scale up.

"The CROPS project examines critical factors — barriers and enablers — that determine whether scaling efforts succeed or stall, drawing on experiences from funding structures, data management systems, and cultural and societal factors."

Restore our Oceans & Waters — Strongest overall

Consistently high across all five dimensions, particularly due to EMODnet and participatory tools like iNaturalist. Highest citizen data submission score of any mission.

Adaptation to Climate Change — Strong technically

Performs well on openness, interoperability and sustainability (Copernicus CDS, GBIF, Climate-ADAPT). However shows minimal citizen contribution — data flows almost entirely from meteorological agencies.

Climate-Neutral Smart Cities — High participation, fragmented

Scores highest on citizen submission (Smart Citizen, OpenStreetMap) but suffers from fragmentation in interoperability and unclear reuse licensing across city-specific platforms.

A Soil Deal for Europe — Robust openness, limited civic participation

Strong openness and sustainability (ESDAC, ISRIC, LUCAS) but lacks citizen contribution mechanisms and clear reuse protocols. Major repositories do not enable direct public submission.

Cancer — Lowest overall

Ethical and legal constraints inherent in health data governance limit openness, citizen submission and licensing clarity across all repositories. PatientsLikeMe accepts citizen data but lacks transparency and institutional sustainability.

Results · D4.1, Section 7

Key findings: strengths and critical gaps

From the comparative analysis of 17 repositories across 5 EU Missions, drawing on the five analytical dimensions and FAIR principles.

What works well

Technical quality

EMODnet, Copernicus CDS and ESDAC show strong FAIR alignment, INSPIRE-compliant metadata, and sustained EU institutional backing. These represent the baseline for mission-critical infrastructure.

Participatory design

iNaturalist and OpenStreetMap demonstrate what best practice looks like when citizen contribution is embedded by design — open licensing, peer validation, and machine-readable APIs from the outset.

Licensing openness

Oceans, Adaptation and Smart Cities missions predominantly use CC0 / CC-BY licences, enabling broad secondary reuse without legal barriers for researchers, policymakers and businesses.

Critical gaps

No citizen submission pathway

Most institutional repositories have no clear public submission pathway. Climate adaptation data flows almost entirely from meteorological agencies — excluding valuable community knowledge.

Licensing inconsistency

Soil and Cancer repositories lack harmonised reuse terms. Inconsistent or ambiguous licensing blocks secondary use and cross-mission data integration.

Sustainability risk

Many citizen science platforms depend on time-bound EU project funding with no continuity plan. When grants end, data and communities can disappear.

Governance opacity

Few repositories publish clear governance frameworks or ethical guidelines. Transparency about who controls data, how it is licensed and how contributors are credited directly drives volunteer trust.

"Most platforms lack clear citizen submission channels, consistent reuse terms, or visible governance frameworks. Participatory platforms such as iNaturalist and OpenStreetMap highlight best practices but are not yet mainstreamed across Missions."

Section 8, D4.1 · Best practices

Best practices for open data repositories

Six evidence-based practices identified from repositories that most effectively support inclusive participation, sustainable data use and mission-relevant research. Drawn directly from D4.1 Section 8.

a
Recognised metadata standards

Adopt domain-relevant schemas — Darwin Core (biodiversity), ISO 19115 (geospatial), INSPIRE (EU spatial data). Enables cross-platform discovery and long-term curation.

Examples: EMODnet, OBIS, ESDAC
b
Machine-readable formats and APIs

CSV, JSON, NetCDF, GeoTIFF plus public APIs for real-time integration into dashboards and climate models. Enables automation, visualisation and third-party innovation.

Examples: OpenStreetMap, Smart Citizen, Copernicus CDS, OpenLandMap
c
Clear and inclusive reuse licensing

CC0, CC-BY or ODbL licences that are prominently displayed and enforced. Reduces legal uncertainty and supports cross-sectoral uptake in research, policy and public engagement.

Examples: Zenodo, GBIF, OpenStreetMap
d
Ethical governance and participatory models

Transparency about data management decisions and contributor rights. Community moderation and peer-validation empower contributors while ensuring data integrity.

Examples: iNaturalist, OpenStreetMap, Cancer Imaging Archive (for sensitive data)
e
Equity and accessibility in design

User-friendly interfaces, multilingual support, and clear pathways for non-expert contributors. Open submission must be balanced with quality safeguards and validation protocols.

Examples: Smart Citizen, EMODnet Biology
f
Alignment with policy frameworks

Repository outputs linked explicitly to EU monitoring instruments — CAP (Soil), Marine Strategy Directive (Oceans), EU Green Deal (Cities/Adaptation), Beating Cancer Plan (Cancer).

Examples: EMODnet → marine policy compliance, ESDAC → CAP monitoring
Section 10, D4.1 · Recommendations

Recommendations for open data ecosystems

Practical roadmap for improving the inclusivity, reliability and long-term impact of open data repositories aligned with EU Mission objectives. From D4.1 Section 10.

  1. 1
    Strengthen cross-mission interoperability

    Adopt common metadata standards (ISO 19115, INSPIRE, Darwin Core) and develop crosswalks to enable data exchange between repositories serving different Missions — horizontal integration across biodiversity, climate and urban domains.

  2. 2
    Mandate open licensing and machine-readable formats

    Require all EU-funded repositories to publish under open licences (CC-BY or CC0) in accessible formats (CSV, JSON, NetCDF) with documented APIs. Promotes reuse, automation and integration across scientific and civic data ecosystems.

  3. 3
    Expand citizen participation pathways

    Develop structured submission channels with quality control protocols and contributor guidance. Participation should be inclusive but not at the expense of data integrity or interoperability.

  4. 4
    Improve governance transparency

    Ensure repositories publish clear governance frameworks, ethical guidelines and user support mechanisms for feedback, dispute resolution and attribution. Particularly vital for repositories handling sensitive or community-generated data.

  5. 5
    Align with policy instruments and monitoring systems

    Design repository outputs with explicit links to EU frameworks — CAP (Soil Deal), Marine Strategy Directive (Oceans), EU Green Deal (Smart Cities/Adaptation), Europe's Beating Cancer Plan (Cancer Mission). Open data must directly inform regulatory action.

  6. 6
    Support long-term sustainability beyond project lifecycles

    Embed data stewardship plans from project outset. Explore institutional, federated or mission-driven hosting models to ensure repositories remain active and reliable beyond EU funding cycles. Essential to prevent data loss and enable cumulative science.
